Shot Selection, Space & Efficiency in the Modern NBA

Over two decades, professional basketball has undergone a quiet revolution — the three-pointer went from a parlor trick to the backbone of every offensive system. This is the story of where shots happen, and why location matters more than almost anything else in the sport.

Basketball is a spatial game. Every possession is a negotiation over territory — who controls which part of the floor, and who converts that control into points.

For most of the league's history, coaches and players operated on intuition and tradition. Certain shots were considered "good" because they always had been. The mid-range pull-up was the hallmark of a skilled scorer. The post-up was how big men dominated. The three-pointer was a weapon of last resort.

Then the math arrived. And it said something uncomfortable: almost everything everyone believed about good shot selection was wrong.

A three-pointer at 33% accuracy yields the exact same expected points per attempt as a two-pointer made at 50%. This one equation — so simple it fits on a napkin — quietly rewired how the smartest front offices in basketball thought about the game. Once you see it, you can't unsee it. And once a few teams acted on it, the rest had no choice but to follow.

"The corner three is the same distance as a wing three but scored differently in every coach's playbook — and yet it converts at rates that rival layups when measured per expected point."

By 2024, 39% of all field goal attempts in the NBA came from beyond the arc — more than double the rate in 2004. Mid-range attempts dropped by more than half. The corner three, once a footnote, became the most coveted shot in the sport. This transformation happened faster than almost any structural shift in the history of professional sports. Here's how it unfolded.

To understand why shot selection changed so dramatically, three ideas need to be in place. They sound simple. Their implications were anything but.

01

Expected Value

A 3-pointer at 33% accuracy generates 0.99 expected points per attempt. A mid-range two at 42% generates 0.84. The gap compounds over thousands of possessions — the equivalent of scoring several extra points per game, every game, all season.

02

Shot Quality

Not all threes are equal. An open corner three from a 40% shooter is a wildly different proposition than a contested pull-up from 28 feet. "Shot quality" captures difficulty, defender proximity, and shooter skill — collapsing them into a single actionable metric.

03

Spacing

When shooters spread to the perimeter, defenders must follow — which tears open driving lanes to the rim. The threat of the three creates the layup. Space isn't just a byproduct of good offense; it's the mechanism that makes the whole system work.

Armed with these ideas, teams started asking a question that nobody had asked systematically before: which zones on the floor actually produce the most points per attempt? The answer, visible in the data, pointed to two places — the rim, and the corners. The vast territory in between, the mid-range, turned out to be a trap.

The clearest way to see this philosophy in action is to look at where individual players actually shoot from. Zone Efficiency

This wing player's shot chart reflects a modern offensive philosophy: heavy three-point volume in the corners and at the break, combined with high-frequency rim attacks. The mid-range is nearly absent by design.

What's striking isn't just where the shots are — it's where they aren't. The mid-range corridor between roughly 10 and 22 feet, once the domain of Kobe Bryant's stepback and Dirk Nowitzki's one-legged fade, is now largely abandoned. Not because players can't make those shots. Because better options exist almost every time, and the data proved it.

Individual shot charts show the philosophy. League-wide trend data shows the reckoning.

From 2004 to 2024, three-point attempt rate more than doubled — from 17% to 39% of all field goal attempts. That's not a gradual drift; it's a structural transformation. And it happened because every year, teams that shot more threes tended to score more efficiently. The feedback loop was clear, and eventually irresistible.

The trend isn't uniform. Teams arrived at these conclusions at different speeds, through different paths — and the outliers are the most revealing part of the story.

The Golden State Warriors of 2015–19 didn't just adopt the analytics playbook; they embodied it so completely that they became the template every other team tried to copy. Stephen Curry and Klay Thompson stretched defenses to their breaking point, turning corner threes into what amounted to automatic points if the defense lost track of them for even a moment.

The Houston Rockets under Daryl Morey took it further — farther than anyone thought was sustainable. In 2018, they essentially eliminated the mid-range by front-office policy, finishing the season with a three-point attempt rate of 45%. They won 65 games. The experiment showed what pure optimization looked like at its extreme.

And then there's the Denver Nuggets — who won the 2023 championship without a dominant three-point shooting profile, built instead around Nikola Jokić's gravitational effect on defenses. Their story is the important counterpoint: the data doesn't mandate specific shot types, it demands efficiency. Jokić found it differently.

"The lesson isn't simply 'shoot more threes.' It's that shot quality compounds. Teams that generate better looks tend to sustain offensive output across the grind of a season — and into the playoffs, where defenses tighten and every possession narrows."

Twenty years of data point in the same direction. The math behind shot selection isn't going away, and defenses haven't found a way to neutralize it that doesn't create a different problem somewhere else on the floor. Basketball is beautiful precisely because it's a game of choices under pressure — and the data has permanently changed which choices the smartest teams make.
